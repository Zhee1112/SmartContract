Abstract—This study investigates the optimization of gas costs and the security of smart contract bridges on a four-tier EIP-1153 transient storage architecture. The rapid growth of cross-chain transactions in recent years demands bridge solutions that are not only gas-efficient but also resilient against attacks such as reentrancy and MEV sandwich. Four bridge architectures are designed: Tier A (no optimization), Tier B (static optimization), Tier C (external dynamic defense), and Tier D (inline dynamic defense). These are compared through empirical measurements using Foundry with 100 samples per operation. Security testing covers eight critical features, while statistical analysis employs Welch's t-test, Cohen's d effect size, and 95% confidence intervals. Results indicate that the inline architecture (Tier D) achieves a perfect security score (8/8) with 72–88% lower gas costs compared to Tier C. The proposed EIP-1153 modification transforms it from a single reentrancy guard into a multifunctional security platform encompassing five defense mechanisms. The on-chain Early Warning System (EWS) proves effective in detecting sandwich attack patterns with a 96% probability. These findings demonstrate that the inline approach offers an optimal balance between gas efficiency and security robustness in cross-chain bridge infrastructure.

Index Terms—bridge blockchain, EIP-1153, gas optimization, smart contract security, transient storage.
